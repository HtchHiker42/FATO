Grammar Notes

Key concepts from Lessons 20–30, straight from the textbook

Aspect

Both perfect and pluperfect have perfective aspect: the action is already completed. The perfect describes a present state resulting from a completed action ("I have won" → I am now a winner). The pluperfect describes a past state resulting from a completed action ("I had won").

Reduplication

The perfect stem is formed by reduplicating (doubling a sound at the start) the basic stem:

  • Rule 1 (single consonant or stop + liquid/nasal): Prefix = initial consonant + ε. E.g., παιδεύω → πε-παιδευκα. If the consonant is aspirated (φ, θ, χ), use the corresponding smooth stop: θύω → τε-θυκα (not θε-).
  • Rule 2 (all other cases): Augment the stem as you would for the aorist. Vowel-initial stems lengthen; stems beginning with ρ double it; stems beginning with ζ or double consonant add ε-.

The κ of the Perfect

The letter κ is characteristic of the perfect active. Verbs whose stems end in a labial or palatal lose the κ (second perfects). A verb with both types usually differs in meaning: e.g., πέπεικα "I have persuaded" vs. πέποιθα "I trust."

Interrogative τίς ("who? what? which?")

Uses third-declension endings. The accent stays acute forever, even where it should become grave (e.g., τίς ἵππος, not τὶς ἵππος). The neuter singular accusative τί used as an adverb means "why?".

Indefinite τις ("someone, something, a/an, some, any")

Same forms as interrogative τίς but is an enclitic — it prefers to come just after the word it qualifies. The contracted forms του (= τινός gen.) and τῳ (= τινί dat.) look like the definite article's gen./dat. masculine/neuter singular.

Vowel-Stem Verbs

Drop the κ from the perfect active stem → add primary M/P endings (-μαι, -σαι, -ται, -μεθα, -σθε, -νται) with no thematic vowel. Infinitive ends in -σθαι.

Dative of Personal Agent

With perfect and pluperfect passive verbs, use a simple dative (no preposition) for the person responsible: ἐμοί τοῦτο πέπρακται = "This has been done by me."

Relative Pronoun ὅς, ἥ, ὅ

Looks like the definite article with the initial τ dropped and rough breathing added. A relative pronoun agrees in gender and number with its antecedent but its case is determined by its function inside the relative clause.

The antecedent may come before or after the relative clause; a demonstrative often serves as antecedent; the antecedent can be omitted entirely.

Expressions of Time

  • Genitive of time within which: action occurs at some point inside the period (ταύτης τῆς ἡμέρας "during that day")
  • Dative of time when: dates the action as a single point (ταύτῃ τῇ ἡμέρᾳ "on that day")
  • Accusative of extent of time: action extends throughout the period (πολλὰς ἡμέρας "for many days")

What is a Participle?

A verbal adjective: it has tense and voice (like a verb), and agrees in gender, number, and case with a noun (like an adjective). Participles are never augmented.

Three Uses of the Participle

  • Attributive: in attributive position; characterizes a noun like a relative clause. "the slave who leads..."
  • Circumstantial: in predicate position; describes circumstances (temporal, causal, concessive, conditional). Particles καίπερ (although), ἅτε (because), ὡς (as if) clarify the relationship.
  • Supplementary: completes the idea of the main verb (introduced in L25). Essential to the sentence's meaning.

Supplementary Participles

Used with verbs like παύω ("stop/cease") and verbs of emotion like χαίρω:

  • παύω αὐτοὺς γράφοντας = "I stop them from writing" (participle modifies object)
  • παύομαι γράφων = "I cease writing" (participle modifies subject)
  • χαίρω γράφουσα = "I take delight in writing"

Periphrastic forms: Perfect M/P participle + εἰσί / ἦσαν = 3rd pl. perfect/pluperfect M/P of consonant-stem verbs.

Indirect Questions — Rules

  1. Retain the same mood and tense as the direct question (Greek doesn't shift tense like English).
  2. Adjust person as needed (1st/2nd person → 3rd person).
  3. If no interrogative word: add εἰ ("whether") at the start.
  4. τίς → ὅστις; π-adverbs → ὁπ- adverbs (ποῦ → ὅπου; πῶς → ὅπως, etc.).

Alternative Questions

  • Direct: πότερον … ἤ ("either … or")
  • Indirect: εἴτε … εἴτε ("whether … or")

Formation

Built on the 6th principal part. Suffix is -θη- / -θε- (1st aorist passive) or -η- / -ε- (2nd aorist passive, no θ). Uses secondary ACTIVE endings (-ν, -ς, —, -μεν, -τε, -σαν) — defying logic!

Consonant Changes Before -θη-

  • Dental drops + σ added: πείθω → ἐπείσθην
  • Labial (β/π) roughens to φ: πέμπω → ἐπέμφθην
  • Palatal (γ/κ) roughens to χ: διώκω → ἐδιώχθην
  • θ before -θη- changes to τ: θύω → ἐτύθην (not ἐθύθην)
  • Contract verbs lengthen: α,ε → η; ο → ω

Key Note

A verb with a 2nd aorist active always has a 1st aorist passive (e.g., λείπω: 2nd aor. act. ἔλιπον → 1st aor. pass. ἐλείφθην).

Future Passive

Same stem as aorist passive (no augment) + σ + thematic vowel + primary passive endings. E.g., παιδευθήσομαι "I shall be taught."

Future Perfect

Usually periphrastic: future of εἰμί + perfect active or M/P participle. Rare exception: ἀποθνῄσκω → τεθνήξω "I shall be dead."

Vowel-Stem Nouns (ι, υ, ευ)

In ι- and υ-stem nouns, quantitative metathesis occurs: η becomes short and ο becomes long in gen. sg. (πόλη-ος → πόλεως). The accent stays on the original syllable despite the shift.

Syncopated Stems (πατήρ, μήτηρ, θυγάτηρ)

The stem-vowel disappears in certain forms. Rules: nom. sg. = lengthened stem (-ηρ); voc. sg. = recessive accent; gen./dat. sg. and dat. pl. = syncopated stem (-ρ, with α inserted before σι in dat. pl.).

Deponent Verbs

Verbs whose form is always middle/passive but whose meaning is always active. Middle deponent: has aorist middle (e.g., ἀφικνέομαι → ἀφῑκόμην). Passive deponent: has aorist passive (e.g., βούλομαι → ἐβουλήθην).

Genitive Absolute

A circumstantial participle + a noun/pronoun in the genitive case, where that noun/pronoun has no grammatical role in the main clause. It floats alongside the main clause expressing time, cause, concession, condition, etc.

τοῦ οἰκέτου ἀνοίγοντος τὴν θύραν, τὸν θησαυρὸν ὄψομαι.
"When the servant opens the door, I shall see the treasure."

Clarifying particles (καίπερ, ἅτε, ὡς) may precede the participle. Subordinating conjunctions (ἐπεί, ἕως, ὅτι) are never used.

εἷς, μία, ἕν ("one")

Mixed declension (3rd for M/N, 1st for F). Accent jumps to ultima in gen. and dat. of all genders. οὐδείς / μηδείς = "no one, nothing" (compound of οὐδέ/μηδέ + εἷς). Double negatives: if compound comes first, the second negative strengthens it; if compound follows οὐ/μή, they cancel to a positive.
